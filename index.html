2026 Season

Champs Fantasy Baseball Draft Recap

All 16 teams reviewed. Grades assigned. No punches pulled.

16-Team Snake Draft 12 Categories 25 Rounds / 400 Picks OPS / SVHD

Sixteen teams, 400 picks, and one snake draft that produced some fascinating roster constructions. This league scores in 12 categories (R, HR, RBI, SB, AVG, OPS for hitters and K, QS, W, ERA, WHIP, SVHD for pitchers) with an 11 GS weekly cap, which makes pitching strategy just as important as the arms you draft. Let's break down every team, starting from the top of the draft board.

BLACKOUT

Pick 1
Shohei Ohtani · #1Best player in fantasy, no debate Jesus Luzardo · #64Elite arm at a mid-round price Connelly Early · #257Young arm, low-cost upside flier Gleyber Torres · #193Solid bat in the 13th round

Taking Ohtani first overall is never wrong. You get the best hitter in fantasy AND a frontline starter in a single roster spot. That dual eligibility is the kind of cheat code that wins leagues. Following up with Logan Gilbert gives this team two aces before anyone else has one.

Jesus Luzardo at pick 64 stands out as the best value grab of this draft class. A frontline arm in the fourth round is a steal, and if he stays healthy, this team has three legitimate top-of-rotation arms. That's a pitching core most teams would kill for.

The problem is everywhere else. The batting order after Ohtani and Machado is a parade of .240 hitters. Ben Rice, Teoscar Hernandez, Willy Adames, and Ozzie Albies are all functional, but none of them scare anyone. In a league that counts both AVG and OPS, running out five regulars below .250 is asking for trouble. Connelly Early at pick 257 and Nolan Gorman are two late picks that probably won't help. And Nasim Nunez is the worst pick in the entire draft.

The RP group of Jeff Hoffman, Robert Garcia, and Hunter Gaddis is thin. This team will be near the bottom in SVHD unless someone emerges on the wire.

B+ Ohtani and Luzardo give this team an elite pitching ceiling. But the lineup is full of average-or-below hitters, and the late rounds were filled with low-ceiling bats that won't help in AVG or OPS. This team needs Ohtani to be superhuman, which, to be fair, he usually is.

We Be Cruz'n

Pick 2
Aaron Judge · #2Best pure power bat in baseball Josh Hader · #290Elite closer buried in round 19 Noelvi Marte · #12780-game PED ban clouds 2026 timeline, just 58 career MLB games Walker Buehler · #383Former ace, round 24 bounce-back dart

Judge at 2 is the right call. He's the best pure power bat in baseball and the OPS upside is unmatched. Pairing him with Bryce Harper in round 2 gives this team two of the best OPS producers on the planet. That's a foundation you can build a championship around.

The RP group is where this draft gets quietly excellent. Josh Hader at pick 290 projects for 626 fantasy points and is one of the best closers in baseball. Bryan Abreu at pick 194 adds another high-leverage arm. That's elite SVHD production from mid-to-late round picks.

But there are some head-scratchers in the middle. Noelvi Marte at pick 127 is coming off a PED suspension with unproven production. Jackson Holliday at pick 159 is another gamble on a player who hasn't figured out big league pitching yet. And Jordan Lawlar at pick 287 is a reach that screams "dynasty mindset in a redraft league." Three high-risk lottery tickets in one draft is aggressive.

The SP staff is solid but lacks a true ace. Hunter Brown, Nolan McLean, and Sonny Gray are all good, but none of them are going to dominate K or ERA on a weekly basis. Walker Buehler in round 24 is a desperation add that probably ends up as a drop by May.

B Judge and Harper are a top-tier offensive pairing, and the bullpen is sneaky strong. But too many mid-round picks were spent on unproven gambles, and the SP staff lacks a frontline arm. The ceiling is high if Holliday and Marte break out, but that's a big if.

The Natural

Pick 3
Bobby Witt Jr. · #3Five-category monster at SS Bubba Chandler · #158Legit SP upside in round 10 Owen Caissie · #323Talented prospect, free round 21 dart 12 SPs Drafted · #—Extreme roster construction

Bobby Witt Jr. is a five-category monster and the perfect anchor for any roster. Jazz Chisholm in round 2 adds another dynamic bat with positional flexibility. Through two rounds, this was one of the best starts in the draft.

Then things got weird. Starting in round 8, The Natural drafted TWELVE starting pitchers. Drew Rasmussen, Sandy Alcantara, Bubba Chandler, Cade Horton, Edward Cabrera, Mike Burrows, Mick Abel, Cade Cavalli, Hunter Greene, Will Warren, Grayson Rodriguez. The strategy is clear: stream matchups and use volume to dominate pitching categories. Bubba Chandler at pick 158 is a legitimate value find.

The cost of that approach? The hitting depth is among the worst in the league. After Witt, Jazz, Rooker, and Naylor, the cupboard is bare. Jo Adell is boom-or-bust with more bust than boom lately. Chandler Simpson is a speed-only asset who won't help in five other categories. Owen Caissie is a reach that could have been spent on a proven bat. And Francisco Alvarez as the second catcher has massive batting average concerns.

The RP situation is an afterthought. Grant Taylor, Steven Okert, and Garrett Cleavinger project for just 14 combined SVHD. In a league where other teams stacked closers, this is essentially throwing a category away.

C+ Witt and Jazz are excellent, and the SP volume strategy is creative. But 12 starting pitchers on a 25-man roster leaves almost no room for hitting depth, and the SVHD production is nonexistent. This team is one Witt injury away from a lost season.

Struggle Bus

Pick 4
Juan Soto · #4Generational OPS machine David Bednar · #132League-best 903 FPTS from a RP Andres Munoz · #125Elite closer, RP strategy begins Shane Smith · #253Solid SP value in round 16

Don't let the name fool you. This is the best-drafted team in the league, and it's not particularly close in terms of having a clear, executable plan.

Soto is a generational OPS machine. Lindor is a five-category contributor. Marte is an AVG/OPS anchor. Duran adds speed. That first four rounds produced an offense that should compete in virtually every hitting category every single week.

Then came the masterstroke. While other teams were chasing mid-tier starters, Struggle Bus loaded up on elite relievers: Andres Munoz at pick 125, David Bednar at pick 132 (projecting a league-best 903 fantasy points), Raisel Iglesias at 189, and Emilio Pagan at 196. The projected 122 SVHD leads the league by a mile. That's not winning a category. That's owning it.

The first SP off the board? Max Scherzer. At pick 228. In round 15. That takes guts. The late-round SP haul of Shane Smith, Reynaldo Lopez, Chad Patrick, and Tyler Mahle are all serviceable arms who can fill an 11 GS cap without killing your ratios.

The only real weakness is ERA and WHIP. Running out Scherzer, Wacha, Kikuchi, and Jacob Lopez means you're accepting some ugly starts. But if the offense carries and the SVHD dominance holds, this team can afford to lose those categories and still win weeks.

A- The best-planned draft in the league. Elite offense, league-leading SVHD, and a clear willingness to punt SP quality in favor of category dominance. The only risk is if the bargain-bin rotation implodes, but the margin for error is wide.

Corbin Barrels

Pick 5
Jose Ramirez · #5Perennial five-category stud Corey Seager · #69Elite talent, injury history looms Anthony Santander · #357Declining but proven, costs nothing in R23 Aaron Nola · #165Ace-caliber arm in round 11

Jose Ramirez at 5 is a great start. He's a perennial five-category stud and exactly the kind of player you want building around. James Wood in round 2 is a bet on upside that could pay off handsomely.

After that, this draft fell apart. Corey Seager at pick 69 is great when healthy, but his injury history makes him a ticking time bomb in a format where you can't afford empty roster spots. More importantly, the picks around him were wasted. Cody Bellinger at pick 60 is a declining asset with diminishing power. Vinnie Pasquantino is fine but unremarkable.

Aaron Nola at pick 165 is a genuine steal, and Zack Wheeler anchors a decent rotation. But those bright spots can't mask the disaster at the back end of this roster. Anthony Santander at pick 357 is trending the wrong direction with declining power and platoon splits. Ryan Mountcastle's one-dimensional bat doesn't walk enough to help. Josh Lowe's streaky production rounds out a bench that's more liability than asset.

Four relievers (Tanner Scott, Trevor Megill, Alex Vesia, Luke Weaver) eat up roster spots that could have gone to actual contributors. The hitting depth beyond the top four is genuinely concerning, with Taylor Ward, Xavier Edwards, and Alejandro Kirk offering replacement-level production.

D+ Ramirez and the Wheeler/Nola combo are strong building blocks, but the late rounds are full of declining bats and one-dimensional profiles. The bench lacks anyone who can contribute across multiple categories, and the offensive depth beyond the top four hitters is the weakest in the league. This team needs a miracle on the waiver wire.

SANDLOT

Pick 6
Ronald Acuna Jr. · #6Five-category ceiling unmatched Cade Smith · #123796 FPTS, elite RP gem in round 8 Zach McKinstry · #315Low-upside utility, round 20 filler Spencer Strider · #91League-winner or IL stash all year

Acuna falling to 6 is a gift, and SANDLOT grabbed it. Even post-ACL, his five-category ceiling is higher than almost anyone else in fantasy. Pairing him with Trea Turner and Roman Anthony gives this team one of the most exciting top-three offensive trios in the league.

The steal of this draft might be Cade Smith at pick 123. A reliever projecting for 796 fantasy points with a 2.89 ERA and 1.01 WHIP in the 8th round is absurd value. Pair him with Griffin Jax and you have a legit SVHD foundation. That's smart drafting.

But the youth movement in the middle rounds is where this gets risky. Konnor Griffin, Addison Barger, Dylan Crews still figuring it out at the MLB level. These aren't bench stashes. These are players this team needs to start. If two of them hit .220, this lineup is in trouble fast. Zach McKinstry and Sal Frelick are late-round picks that offer nothing.

The SP staff is boom-or-bust incarnate. Spencer Strider returning from injury is either a league-winning pick or an IL stash all year. Roki Sasaki is the same story. Dylan Cease and Matthew Boyd provide stability, but the variance on this rotation is enormous.

B- The Acuna/Anthony/Turner trio is electric, and Cade Smith is a draft-day steal. But the depth is thin, the young bats are unproven, and the SP staff is a rollercoaster. This team has top-3 upside and bottom-5 floor.

THE BIG Bambinos

Pick 7
Tarik Skubal · #7Best pitcher in fantasy, full stop Mason Miller · #58RP in round 4, Diaz went 28 picks later Xander Bogaerts · #282Sneaky value if healthy Matt McLain · #154Shoulder surgery cost him nearly all of 2024, long recovery arc

Skubal at 7 is defensible. He's the best pitcher in fantasy and he'll single-handedly win you pitching categories on a weekly basis. Bryan Woo in round 3 pairs perfectly, giving this team two elite-ratio arms with sub-1.10 WHIPs. That's a dominant foundation.

But then came the decision that defines this draft. Mason Miller at pick 58. In the 4th round. For a reliever. Edwin Diaz, who projects for 974 fantasy points compared to Miller's 797, went 28 picks later at pick 86. Then Jhoan Duran at pick 90. That's two of the first 90 picks spent on relievers, which is a massive investment of draft capital that could have gone to impact bats this team desperately needed.

And the hitting shows it. Tyler Soderstrom, Andy Pages, Matt McLain (pick 154), and Jakob Marsee are a below-average offensive core. The projected 212 HR is second-lowest in the league. Rhys Hoskins in round 21 isn't solving anything. The bright spot is Xander Bogaerts in round 18, a sneaky pickup if he's healthy.

The SP depth behind Skubal and Woo is also thin. Cam Schlittler and Kodai Senga have upside but carry real risk. Brandon Pfaadt and Randy Vasquez are back-end starters at best.

C Skubal and Woo are a dream pitching tandem, and the RP investment will produce SVHD. But two top-90 picks on relievers gutted the offensive depth. This team has the best 1-2 pitching punch in the league and one of the worst lineups. You can't win 12 categories with 6.

Big Blue Nation

Pick 8
Corbin Carroll · #8Elite five-category speed/power Josh Bell · #377Late-round gem, legit production Pete Crow-Armstrong · #57Speed-only, sub-.240 AVG risk Mark Vientos · #217Power-only profile, .242 AVG and poor plate discipline limit category value

Carroll at 8 is the right pick. His speed and improving barrel rates make him a genuine five-category threat. But what happened after that is hard to defend.

Cristopher Sanchez AND Logan Webb in rounds 2-3? Neither is a strikeout pitcher. In a league that counts K as a category, drafting two contact-oriented pitchers with your second and third picks is a strategic misfire. You need arms that miss bats, and these two don't. Joe Ryan in round 5 adds another SP, meaning three of the first five picks were pitchers. That's a lot of early capital on a position with an 11 GS weekly cap.

The hitter group outside Carroll is one of the most underwhelming in the league. Pete Crow-Armstrong is a speed asset who projects for a .235 AVG and .711 OPS. Geraldo Perdomo, Dansby Swanson, Lawrence Butler, and Bryson Stott are all floor-type players who won't hurt you but won't win you any categories either. The projected 231 HR and 801 RBI are near the bottom of the league.

Josh Bell at pick 377 is excellent late-round value and could genuinely help the lineup. But Mark Vientos in round 14 is a power-only profile whose .242 AVG drags down the lineup, and Spencer Steer's production has stalled. The RP group (Abner Uribe, Tyler Rogers, Kevin Ginkel, Brendon Little, Graham Ashcraft) projects for just 30 SVHD, which is near the league basement.

C- Carroll is great, and Josh Bell is a late-round gem. But the pitching-heavy early strategy left this team without enough offensive firepower, and the RP group is nearly nonexistent. Too many safe, low-ceiling picks when this roster needed upside.

Flint Michigan Tropics

Pick 9
Julio Rodriguez · #9Power-speed combo still elite Aroldis Chapman · #184704 FPTS, elite closer in round 12 Coby Mayo · #280Top prospect stash, round 18 upside play Blake Snell · #137Vintage season upside in round 9

Julio at 9 works. The power-speed combination is still elite, and a full healthy season puts him back in the top-5 OF conversation. Yamamoto in round 2 is a solid SP anchor. Devers in round 3 brings the power.

The four-closer strategy is the defining feature of this team. Aroldis Chapman at pick 184 projecting for 704 FPTS is a steal. Carlos Estevez, Kenley Jansen, and Robert Suarez round out a bullpen that should produce 94 SVHD, which is second in the league. That's a smart investment that gives this team a built-in weekly advantage.

But the offense outside the top three is rough. CJ Abrams in round 4 doesn't walk, doesn't hit for average, and doesn't contribute in OPS in a league that counts it. Eugenio Suarez in round 5 is a below-average 3B play. Brandon Lowe's best days are behind him. Coby Mayo in round 18 is one of the worst picks in the draft. Multiple lineup regulars project below .240, which will make AVG a weekly loss.

Blake Snell at pick 137 has vintage season upside, and the SP group of Yamamoto, Gore, Snell, and Bibee is actually quite good. But the offense needs Julio and Devers to both play 150+ games or this team sinks.

C+ The four-closer strategy is smart and the SP group is solid. But the offensive depth outside the top three is among the weakest in the league, and too many hitters project for sub-.240 averages. A SVHD advantage doesn't help when you're losing every hitting category.

Elly at 10 makes sense for the SB upside, but let's be real: a .249 AVG and .741 OPS from your first-round pick is painful in a league that counts both categories. Chourio in round 2 is the same story, tantalizing tools with production that hasn't arrived yet. This team drafted potential over production, and that's a risky bet.

Max Fried is a legitimate ace and the best pick this team made. Framber Valdez adds another quality arm. But the pitching depth drops off fast after that. Andrew Painter at pick 151 is a reach on a pitcher who hasn't proven he can stay healthy. Braxton Ashcraft and Joey Cantillo are deep league filler, not difference makers.

Munetaka Murakami at pick 170 is a nice value grab, bringing power from 3B with legitimate 30+ HR upside. That's the kind of pick that can anchor a lineup. But the rest of the offense is rough. Carson Benge and Chase DeLauter are two significant reaches in the late-middle rounds. The RBI projection of 796 is near the league basement.

The RP group (Seranthony Dominguez, Will Vest, Riley O'Brien, JoJo Romero) projects for 52 SVHD, which is middling. No category dominance anywhere on this roster.

C- Fried and Murakami are the bright spots, but too much of this roster is built on unproven youth and players who haven't produced at the MLB level yet. When your top two picks both project below .250 AVG in a league that counts it, you're starting every week in a hole.

Lil' Sluggers

Pick 11
Kyle Tucker · #11Elite five-category OF anchor Edwin Diaz · #86974 FPTS, best RP in the league Cam Smith · #278Young prospect, round 18 upside dart Carlos Rodon · #171Quality SP value in round 11

Tucker and Alonso give this team a powerful offensive foundation. Tucker is an elite five-category hitter, and Alonso in Baltimore should see a boost in counting stats. Chris Sale in round 3 is a bet on the veteran lefty that carries risk but also carries a Cy Young ceiling. Austin Riley at pick 54 is strong value if he stays on the field.

The draft-defining pick here is Edwin Diaz in round 6 at pick 86. He projects for 974 fantasy points, the highest of ANY reliever in the league, with a 3.16 ERA and 1.05 WHIP. Getting that kind of production from round 6 is a masterstroke. Pair him with Daniel Palencia (694 FPTS at pick 203) and this team has a dominant 1-2 RP combination.

The SP depth is where Lil' Sluggers separates from the pack. Sale, Bradish, Imanaga, Carlos Rodon (pick 171), Bubic, Jack Leiter, Justin Steele, and Kyle Harrison means this team will never lack for quality arms under the 11 GS cap.

The weakness is the bench. Cam Smith in round 18 is an unproven arm without an established MLB role. Carlos Narvaez is a backup catcher ceiling who won't contribute meaningful counting stats. Jeremy Pena, Ceddanne Rafaela, and Jake Burger are all low-upside bats filling starting spots. If Riley goes down, the offensive depth evaporates.

B+ The Tucker/Alonso/Riley offensive core, elite SP depth, and Diaz as a dominant closer make this one of the most complete rosters in the league. The bench is weak and Riley's health is a risk, but the starting lineup is built to win.

Get DUMP-ed

Pick 12
Fernando Tatis Jr. · #12Power-speed elite at OF Gavin Williams · #117Quality SP arm in round 8 Ernie Clement · #213Utility bat with no standout tool, .260/.300 slash won't move the needle Cal Raleigh · #21Clear #1 fantasy catcher

Tatis and Cal Raleigh in the first two rounds is a power-speed combination that sets up nicely for this format. Raleigh is the clear #1 catcher in fantasy, and getting him at pick 21 is a value play that pays dividends all season. Freeman in round 3 adds stability and a high batting average. Strong start.

The SP staff is genuinely deep. Gausman, Eovaldi, Chase Burns, Gavin Williams (pick 117), Shane Baz, and Max Meyer give this team quality arms for every week. Under the 11 GS cap, they'll have the luxury of sitting bad matchups. That's a competitive advantage most teams don't have.

Where this draft stumbles is the middle-class hitters. The 2B/3B spots are a black hole. Isaac Paredes in round 12, Ernie Clement in round 14, and Brendan Donovan at round 13 are all underwhelming solutions. The team clearly prioritized corner bats and pitching but left a crater in the middle infield. Chase Meidroth in the last round doesn't fix it.

The SVHD situation is a real problem. Adrian Morejon, Jason Adam, Gabe Speier, and Clayton Beeter project for just 21 combined SVHD. That's second-worst in the league. In a 12-category format, punting an entire category is a strategic disadvantage that's hard to overcome.

B- Tatis, Raleigh, and Freeman form a great top three, and the SP depth is among the league's best. But the middle infield is a wasteland, the SVHD punt is painful, and the roster lacks the kind of mid-round value picks that separate good drafts from great ones.

RehabsForQuitters

Pick 13
Yordan Alvarez · #20Best pure hitter in this draft George Springer · #84Steal of the draft, veteran producing Evan Carter · #308Talented young bat, free round 20 flier Devin Williams · #173Elite closer anchors the RP group

Full disclosure: this is the team I know the most about, so I'll try to be fair.

The Kurtz/Alvarez combination is high-risk, high-reward. Alvarez at pick 20 is arguably the best pure hitter in this entire draft. Kurtz at 13 is a rookie with elite OPS projections but zero MLB track record. If both hit, this is a top-3 offense. If Kurtz struggles with the adjustment, you've spent your first-round pick on a question mark.

George Springer at pick 84 is the steal of this draft. A veteran who still produces across the board at a pick where most teams were chasing younger, flashier names. That's disciplined drafting that should pay immediate dividends.

The pitching is solid but not spectacular. Ragans is a borderline ace. Eury Perez has ace upside but hasn't proven durability. Cody Ponce, Jack Flaherty, and Jose Soriano are all fine mid-rotation arms. Devin Williams at pick 173 anchors a solid RP group, and Ryan Walker adds depth. That's a respectable SVHD floor.

The concerns are real though. Kazuma Okamoto at 3B is a complete unknown in his first MLB season. Trevor Story is a health risk. And the late rounds produced some ugly picks: Evan Carter is a sophomore slump candidate who struggled with breaking balls, Lucas Erceg is a middle reliever without a closer path, and Jordan Hicks is still figuring out the SP workload. If Kurtz or Alvarez miss time, the offensive drop-off is steep.

B Alvarez and Springer give this team a proven offensive core, and the pitching is balanced. But the Kurtz gamble at pick 13 and the Okamoto unknown at 3B add real risk. The late rounds were messy. This team's ceiling is top-3 in the league, but the floor is much lower than it should be.

Joe Buck Yourself

Pick 14
Paul Skenes · #14Best young arm in baseball Joe Musgrove · #270Elite ratios buried in round 17 Mookie Betts · #51Former 1st-rounder at pick 51 SVHD: 5 Projected · #—League-worst, complete forfeit

Skenes at 14 is defensible but comes with opportunity cost. Taking a pitcher in the first round when Tatis, Seager, and Springer were still on the board means you need the rest of the draft to fill the offensive gaps. Schwarber in round 2 helps with elite power and OPS.

Mookie Betts at pick 51 is excellent value for a player who was a first-rounder recently. Even at his age, he contributes across all categories. Wyatt Langford in round 3 is a growth stock with five-category upside. That's a strong offensive trio.

The SP depth is this team's crown jewel. Skenes, Pivetta, Woodruff, Lodolo, Abbott, Mitch Keller, Joe Musgrove (pick 270, elite ratios), and Cristian Javier. That's arguably the deepest rotation in the league. Under the 11 GS cap, this team picks matchups like they're ordering off a menu.

But here's the elephant in the room: 5 projected SVHD. Five. Matt Strahm, Bryan King, and Dylan Lee are the entire bullpen, and none of them are closers. That's not a punt. That's a forfeit. In a 12-category league, giving away an entire category every single week is a significant handicap that even the best rotation in the world can't overcome. This team is playing with 11 categories while everyone else plays with 12.

B- The deepest rotation in the league and a solid offensive core make this a competitive team. But the complete absence of SVHD production is a self-inflicted wound that limits the ceiling. You can win 7 categories and still lose your week when you're gifting one away.

Iowa Barnstormers

Pick 15
Gunnar Henderson · #15Power, speed, positional value at SS Vladimir Guerrero Jr. · #18Best AVG hitter in baseball Jose Caballero · #175Sub-.220 AVG tanks batting categories, speed alone doesn't offset the drag Gerrit Cole · #178Veteran ace stabilizer in round 12

Henderson and Vlad in rounds 1-2 might be the best two-pick start in the entire draft. Henderson brings power, speed, and positional value from SS. Vlad is the best pure batting average hitter in baseball and an OPS monster. That combination alone could carry multiple offensive categories.

Peralta and Kirby in rounds 3-4 lock in a respectable rotation. Gerrit Cole at pick 178 is a nice veteran stabilizer, and Reid Detmers and Ryan Weathers add depth. The pitching is well-constructed.

The problem is that the rest of the offense completely undermines the Henderson/Vlad foundation. Oneil Cruz projects for a .230 AVG and strikes out at an alarming rate. Mike Trout at pick 111 hasn't played a full season in years. Spencer Torkelson is stuck in neutral. Jose Caballero at pick 175 carries a projected .218 AVG, and that kind of AVG drag tanks your batting categories weekly. Carter Jensen, Brett Baty, and Jac Caglianone are all unproven.

The offensive projections tell the story: 708 R, 693 RBI, 207 HR. Those are the worst in the league. The worst. Two MVP-caliber hitters and the worst counting stats in the league. That's how bad the supporting cast is. Six relievers eating bench spots means there's no room to carry extra bats for lineup flexibility.

C Henderson and Vlad are a dream pairing wasted on a roster that can't support them. The worst R, RBI, and HR projections in the league despite having two elite bats tells you everything. Too many roster spots spent on relievers and too many empty-average hitters dragging down the offense.

Salomon's Power

Pick 16
Garrett Crochet · #16High upside, workload risk in Boston Yandy Diaz · #144Underrated AVG/OPS anchor Corbin Burnes · #368Former Cy Young, round 23 bounce-back dart Luisangel Acuna · #337Speed prospect, free round 22 dart throw

Crochet and deGrom in rounds 1 and 3 is a bold pitching-first strategy. Both are high-upside arms, but both carry significant risk. Crochet is adjusting to a full SP workload in Boston, and deGrom is coming off yet another injury. If you're going to spend your first and third picks on pitchers, they need to both be healthy. History suggests that's unlikely.

Yandy Diaz at pick 144 is the best value pick on this roster. He's a batting average and OPS anchor who doesn't get the respect he deserves. Junior Caminero in round 2 has breakout potential at 3B. Zach Neto adds a solid SS. Those are the positives.

The negatives are significant. Byron Buxton (health), Luis Robert Jr. (health), Randy Arozarena (steep decline). That's three outfielders you can't count on to play 130 games. Samuel Basallo in round 11 is a rookie catcher gamble. Josh Smith and Luisangel Acuna are two of the worst picks in the league.

And here's the strange part: this team has NINE starting pitchers but only projects for 14 SVHD. The RP group (Jeremiah Estrada, Garrett Whitlock, Camilo Doval, Phil Maton) isn't bad, but the SVHD output doesn't match the investment. Corbin Burnes at pick 368 raises the question of whether his best days are behind him.

C- Yandy Diaz is a nice value find, and the SP depth is enormous. But this roster is built on health gambles, and none of the three outfielders (Buxton, Robert, Arozarena) can be counted on. The offensive projections are below average, and the late rounds are stacked with health risks and unproven bats.
